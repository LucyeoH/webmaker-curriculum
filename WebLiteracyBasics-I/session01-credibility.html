Reading the Web: Credibility

Made by MOUSE and remixed for Clubs by the #TeachTheWeb.

Learners will use the Internet to solve the mystery of The Kraken, a legendary sea creature, while also learning about search terms, keywords, and how to assess the validity and relevance of web sources.

  • 5
    min

    Preparation

    Print out Legit-O-Meter Worksheets. Get three pieces of blank paper and label them with “Real,” “Imaginary” and “Something In-between” then hang them on different sides of the room.

  • 10
    min

    Introduction Round

    Start a quick conversation about web sources by asking questions like:

    • Is everything you read or watch online true?
    • How do you know when information on the internet is reliable?
    • How can you tell when it isn’t reliable?

    Briefly review the Legit-o-Meter Tip Sheet to discuss some general rules for determining the credibility of a website.

  • 30
    min

    Kraken the Code

    1. Split learners into groups of 2 or 3 and explain that each group’s challenge is to prove whether or not the legendary monster, The Kraken, is real or a myth.

    2. Groups start by spending 20 minutes researching. Using the guidelines from the Tip Sheet , groups should evaluate 4 internet sources, documenting their findings on the Legit-o-Meter (either by writing on the handout or editing the online template). The Legit-o-meter template helps learners track and evaluate the legitimacy of their sources through prompts like:
      • Does the site list an author?
      • When was the information written?
      • What are the article’s sources?
      • Is the site well designed?
    3. At the end of the research period, groups will stand under either the “Real,” “Imaginary,” or “Something Inbetween” sign.
    4. Ask each group what they found out about the Kraken and to defend their opinions using the Legit-o-Meter worksheets they filled out.
    5. After each group has shared and debated, allow participants to switch their position (if they want).

  • 5
    min

    Reflect and Review

    Ask learners to demonstrate what they've learned. You can also ask learners to reflect on what they're doing during the main activity. Ways of doing this include:

    • Asking learners individually to talk you through the process of searching for credible information.
    • Going through a sample search together and asking the group what to do next.
    • Pairing-up learners and asking them to create a screencast showing and verbalising the decisions they make.
